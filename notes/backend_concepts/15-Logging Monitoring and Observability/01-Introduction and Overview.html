Introduction to Observability

Understanding the landscape of tracking, measuring, and debugging modern distributed systems.

Logging, monitoring, and observability constitute a vast domain. There are no strict rules—it is more of a spectrum of practices. No single system follows every single "good practice," but understanding these methodologies is essential to avoid being intimidated by the myriad of keywords, tools, and practices in the industry.

Why Do We Need This?

Modern applications and full-stack backends no longer run in isolation. They are highly distributed:

  • Running on multiple servers.
  • Deployed across different global regions.
  • Serving a worldwide user base.

Given these scenarios, we need practices and tools to keep track of what's happening across all our services and infrastructure. Without them, identifying the root cause of an outage or a performance bottleneck is nearly impossible.

The Core Concepts

To keep our applications highly available, we track the following parameters:

  • Logging: Recording important, suspicious, or security-related events across the lifecycle of a request, tagged with useful metadata (like User IDs or latencies).
  • Monitoring: Keeping track of the real-time state of applications—checking server CPU, memory, active database connections, and tracking historical patterns over time.
  • Observability: Determining the internal state of a complex system purely by examining its external outputs (logs, metrics, and traces).

A Shift from Traditional Monitoring

A decade ago, backend debugging heavily relied on traditional monitoring. We set up alerts, and when a threshold was crossed, we were notified. However, monitoring only tells you that there is a problem.

The modern movement towards Observability goes a step further. While it will inform you that something is wrong, given that you have correctly implemented its three pillars (Logs, Metrics, and Traces), it will also tell you exactly what is wrong.

Mental Model

Think of Monitoring as the dashboard in your car—it tells you the engine is overheating. Think of Observability as popping the hood with advanced diagnostic tools that tell you exactly which valve is malfunctioning.

Important Terms

Spectrum of Practices: Observability isn't a simple binary (yes or no); systems implement these tools and rules to varying degrees.

Distributed Systems: The complex environment of modern backends where services are spread globally, making tracking crucial.

Key Takeaways

  • No system has "perfect" observability; it's a spectrum.
  • Monitoring reveals a problem; Observability reveals the root cause.
  • Tracking is essential for globally distributed architecture.

Quick Revision

  • Logs: Events with metadata.
  • Metrics: Real-time and historical states.
  • Traces: Request transactions across boundaries.
